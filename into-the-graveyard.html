Into The Graveyard

There’s a supermarket close to where I live in Paris. And there’s a graveyard that needs to be traversed to reach the supermarket. The first time I walked into that graveyard, I was surprised at the glorification of death. I had never witnessed such a thing in India. Decorated coffins, with flower pots, some coloured, some having small messages from family, pictures of the deceased. The coffins were so close, the place was a lot denser than I expected. Reminded me that a lot of people die, in fact, one or the other day, everyone does. I still don’t know if the place was dense because there never is enough space for the dead.

One of the days, I happened to diverge from the concrete road in the graveyard and I passed through the graves. Watching through the dates and calculating the ages of those in them, I passed by the graves of French martyrs. What really caught my attention was a grave dated 2012-2014. It had toys, and flowers and most of all, a child in it. I could not help but feel helpless at the thought. Even though I strongly believe that everything is destined, how could this be anyone’s destiny?

The graveyard was in fact the most colourful place around here. I don’t know why, but that just strikes me as ironical. Each person lying there must have held countless stories. Some might have died peacefully, in the arms of their loved ones, some might not have had a final goodbye. What about the warriors? And what about the children? Can there ever be a goodbye to a child? Death is full of mysteries, that’s true, but it’s also ironical in most ways.

When I went shopping a couple of days ago, I realized it might be the last time I enter that place, so I took more time than usual. Some graves were broken. Some flower pots had fallen due to the wind. For the first time I noticed that there were a line of small graves, a shiver ran through my spine. A sense of satisfaction ran through my body when I saw a grave with a couple’s name, both in their 80s. They must have had a full happy life, or so I hoped. I also noticed that some graves were not so decorated, plain. Maybe their families weren't so rich, or maybe they didn't have a family? That place has a feeling to it, a pain, something cold, but it also has the warmth of love, love beyond life. Who knows where the dead go? Whether they remember us or not. But with those who live, the love stays. I can see it in the eyes of people who visit.

Each grave has a story, but it doesn't tell it, at least not out loud. People like me are outsiders, we are people who guess. It is one of the most humbling places I set foot into. And the biggest truth I learnt. That death doesn't speak. It only silences.
