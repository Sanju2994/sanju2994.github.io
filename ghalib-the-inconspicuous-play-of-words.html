Ghalib: The Inconspicuous Play of Words

By Arpit Agrawal

While wordplay is not rare to find in Ghalib’s verses or Urdu verse in general, a significant portion of the wordplay is inaccessible to the layman because it requires a working knowledge of the Persian language.

Unlike English which follows a somewhat strict subject-verb-object sequence in sentences, Hindi-Urdu typically follows a subject-object-verb sequence. While the nouns at the end of English sentences are easy to rhyme, the verb endings in Urdu can only be repeated, hence in most Urdu poetry, the poetic element comes from refrain rather than rhyme (there is rhyme too, but that’s buried somewhere within the lines). This imposes a severe constraint on the mood and tense used in the verse. Since each couplet of the ghazal would typically end with a verb and the end of these verbs must be the same, usually, the whole ghazal ends up using the same tense and mood.

For instance, the first ghazal below is in a declarative tone with each line ending in hazaron hain, the second one is a prescriptive one ending with chahiye, while the third one by Mir Taqi Meer is declarative past tense.

In aankhon ki masti ke mastaane hazaaron hain
In aankhon se vaabasta afsaane hazaaron hain

Ho gayi hai peer parvat si pighalni chahiye
Is himalay se koi ganga nikalni chahiye

Ulti ho gayi sab tadbeerein kuch na dava ne kaam kiya
Dekha us beemari-e-dil ne aakhir kaam tamaam kiya

And here comes Ghalib

One of the more popular ghazals by Ghalib, however, ceases to bow down to this linguistic handicap. The couplet below, although harmless in plain sight to a native Urdu speaker, plays with the grammar of the language to use the same word ending to imply two different moods.

hazāroñ ḳhvāhisheñ aisī ki har ḳhvāhish pay dam niklay
bahut niklay mire armān lekin phir bhī kam niklay

While both the lines end with the word niklay, the meaning of the word is very different in the two contexts. In the first line, niklay is used in the singular subjunctive sense, to refer to a conditional situation (the corresponding tense for the verb nikal jana would be nikal jaaye). In the second line, niklay is used in the declarative simple past (had he used nikal jana instead of nikalna, the form would have been nikal gaye). Throughout the ghazal, Ghalib keeps switching between the two connotations of niklay avoiding the usual sense of monotony among the couplets. In the two couplets listed below, both end with a different tense.

nikalnā ḳhuld se aadam kā sunte aa.e haiñ lekin
bahut be-ābrū ho kar tire kūche se ham nikle

bharam khul jaa.e zālim tere qāmat kī darāzī kā
agar is turra-e-pur-pech-o-ḳham kā pech-o-ḳham nikle

But should one care about it?

While wordplay is not rare to find in Ghalib’s verses or Urdu verse in general, a significant portion of the wordplay is inaccessible to the layman because it requires a working knowledge of the Persian language. Consider the couplet below where Ghalib plays on the dual meaning of the word dimaaG, nose and brain. Since an average Urdu speaker would only know that dimaaG means brain, he wouldn’t be able to appreciate the wordplay in the verse below.

Muhabbat thi chaman say lekin ab yeh bedimaaGi hay
keh mauj-e-bu-e-gul say naak mein aata hay dam mera

However, the play of words in verb tenses is accessible to everyone since it doesn’t require any particular education of Persian or even Urdu. It doesn’t need a tonne of vocabulary that can only be acquired by years of reading by the elite few. Even the layman who hears the poem sung on the streets of the market can interpret it and can appreciate how the moods change through the verses in the ghazal. Ghalib’s genius thus lies in not just his command over the languages of the elite, but also in wordplay that can appeal to the masses.
