But if you never ask, how will you ever know?

Most of us live in the fear of speaking the truth. We spend our lives lying through our teeth covering for our shortcomings-or what we think are shortcomings.

We learn to be what society wants us to be. We spend years trying to live up to someone else’s idea of greatness, someone else’s projection of what our future should look like, someone else’s decision of what our careers are supposed to be, someone else’s choice of our life partner. We tell ourselves that we’re not equipped enough to make big choices, big decisions that could turn out wrong, that someone else knows better-our parents, our bosses, our neighbours, our teachers, our priests-people with more experience, people who know better, people who’ve seen more, lived more. We live driven by the force of not wanting to repeat their mistakes, treading carefully, suppressing that inner voice that screams, “I don’t want this.” We tell it to be quiet, till it dies, and after a point, we can’t really differentiate between what we actually want to say and what we’re supposed to say.

We learn to fake to love our jobs, we go around telling people how much it inspires us, motivates us. Most people are not inspired. But we lie. We learn to live with partners we don’t love because our parents chose them. Because a thousand people saw and liked our candid pre-wedding shoot on facebook. How could we not be in love? We’re supposed to, right? And then, we never admit. We just live the lie.

We are terrified to ask for better. We are terrified of not settling, because what if there is nothing better? We are terrified to ask for a better job because of the fear of not being good enough, not being worth it, being shot down and rejected. All of us have strengths, and more likely than not, statistically, there’s a job out there that plays to our strengths. But month on month, year on year, we tell ourselves that it’s okay, because everyone else is doing it, and they’re lying too.

We’re taught to not be vocal of our real desires, lest it offend someone. Everyone asks us to be ‘honest’, but what they really want is a toned down version of honesty mixed with some diplomacy that amounts to the ‘right answer’ on the scale of nobody taking offence.

It’s hard to be honest, because so many people don’t appreciate honesty as much as they say they do. But it’s also important to be honest, otherwise it becomes insanely difficult after a point to even recognise the voice that comes out of your mouth. Is it really me? I ask myself often during important conversations, is that what I really really want to say or is that what I’m supposed to say? It sucks how many times it’s the second. I try. It doesn’t always get me the best response, it reveals my vulnerabilities, but it also reveals my strengths. I’m not afraid to be who I am, because it will lead me to somewhere I’m meant to be. If I can’t voice my unhappiness today, I’m doomed to be unhappy. The other way-at least there’s some chance of finding happiness, meaning or whatever it is that we mortal beings look for.

So if there’s something you want, something you would like to change, ask. Take a stand. Maybe someone who’s both nice and powerful will help you get there. But if you never ask, how will you ever know?
